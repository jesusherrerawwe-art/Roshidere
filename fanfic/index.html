Episode 1 — “The MVP and the Invitation That Crits”

The MVP had told himself he would treat today like a tutorial stage: breathe, don’t overthink, don’t read romance into every accidental eye contact. Then the universe laughed in his face and rolled a natural twenty.

Otobuki’s evening campus looked unfairly cinematic—streetlamps painting long stripes across the quad, vending machines humming like polite NPCs, the kind of lighting that made ordinary people look like they had secret routes. The MVP was not “ordinary,” exactly; he was the guy who carried a handheld in his jacket like a security blanket and treated co-op queues like sacred promises. He liked games the way some people liked oxygen: quietly, constantly, and with a devotion that didn’t always translate into “cool.”

Which was why, when a voice cut through his headphone-adjacent bubble, his first instinct was to assume he’d misclicked reality.

“Excuse me,” she said—and the word excuse had no business sounding that smooth. “You’re The MVP, right? Second-year, evening program?”

He looked up and immediately understood why people used phrases like “unfair character design.” Karen Tendō stood there in the student union’s spill of warm light, blazer crisp, big ribbon bow perfectly cocky, blonde hair catching gold like she had her own personal key light. She smiled like she’d practiced being approachable in a mirror and still somehow meant it.

“Uh,” he said, eloquently.

Her eyes—bright green in the anime posters of his brain, sharp and alive in person—flicked to the handheld peeking from his pocket, then back to his face with a spark of delight that felt dangerously personal. “You’re a gamer,” she said, not like an accusation. Like a discovery.

“I’m… a hobbyist,” he managed, because “yes, I have opinions about frame data and also cry at good soundtracks” felt like too much too soon.

Karen stepped half a pace closer. Not into his space—just close enough that he caught the faint scent of citrus and something warmer, like paper fresh off a printer and promises. Close enough that his brain briefly blue-screened on the sheer presence of her. “I’m recruiting for the Game Club,” she said, voice dropping into something conspiratorial, like she was sharing a cheat code. “We’re trying to rebuild it properly. Competitive integrity. Team drills. Real tournaments—not just… hanging out.”

He swallowed. “That sounds serious.”

“It is,” she agreed, and the way her gaze traced his face felt almost like a touch—slow, deliberate, appreciative in a way that tightened his chest in a wholly inconvenient direction. “Serious can be fun, though. Fun can be… intense.”

Somewhere inside him, a tiny alarm labeled do not flirt back with the pretty club president on day one went off. He ignored it with the same discipline he used when ignoring sleep for a new patch.

She tilted her head. “Come see it tonight. Just a look. If you hate it, I’ll accept your decline with grace.” A beat. Her smile turned a fraction sharper, more playful. “If you love it, I’ll pretend I knew all along.”

The club room smelled like plastic, energy drinks, and competitive sweat—the honest musk of people who cared too much about pixels. Monitors lined a long table like a shrine. Someone was warming up on a fighting game; someone else was drilling inputs on a rhythm title with the focus of a surgeon.

Karen moved through it like she belonged to the room and the room belonged to her. She introduced him with a flourish that made his ears hot: “Everyone—fresh blood. Potential carry.”

“I’m not a carry,” he muttered, too late.

“Not yet,” she murmured, sidling beside him while the others shouted encouragement at the screen. Her shoulder brushed his upper arm—accident, maybe, except she didn’t flinch away. The contact was small. It still sent a clean little spark down his nerves, the kind of spark that made him hyperaware of his own breathing.

They put a controller in his hands. A shooter. Fast. Loud. The kind of game that turned polite strangers into yelling teammates and smiling rivals. He played for fun, always had—liked the stories, the music, the weird little systems—but he could still click heads when he focused. Halfway through the match, he realized Karen was watching him more than the screen.

After, when the room buzzed with post-game adrenaline, she leaned in near his ear—still not inappropriate, still public, still somehow obscene to his heartbeat. “See?” she whispered. “You shine when you stop apologizing for enjoying the win.”

He turned his head. Their faces were closer than strategy demanded. Close enough that he could count freckles he hadn’t earned the right to count. Close enough that her lips parted, just slightly, like she’d been caught mid-thought and the thought was dangerous.

For a second, the room’s noise dialed down to a muffled hum. He felt the warmth radiating from her skin, saw the quick flutter of her lashes when she didn’t look away. It would’ve been so easy to lean in—wrong context, wrong club, wrong everything—and they both knew it, which was why the tension tasted like candy and static.

She straightened first—professional, smooth—though a faint flush painted her cheeks. “So,” she said, brighter, like resetting a scene. “Official pitch: join us. Weekly drills. scrims. Events.”

His mouth answered before his coward brain could veto. “I… don’t think I’m built for that version of gaming.” He gestured at the monitors, at the trophies glinting on a shelf like sharpened teeth. “I love games. I just… I don’t want them to become a job I have to win to deserve.”

Silence hit the room like a dropped connection.

Karen blinked. For the first time since she appeared in his evening like a walking banner pull, her composure cracked—just a hairline fracture, but he saw it. Surprise. A flicker of something that looked oddly like hurt, quickly masked.

“Oh,” she said softly.

He immediately wanted to rewind, save-scum, choose any dialogue option except the one that made her face do that. “I didn’t mean—”

“No, no.” She lifted a hand, smile snapping back into place like armor—still pretty, now a little brittle at the edges. “That’s fair. Truly.” Her voice steadied, but her fingers tightened around the strap of her club bag until knuckles pale. “If you ever change your mind, the offer stays.”

He left with the memory of her standing in the doorway, backlit, watching him go like she was memorizing a loss screen. And he hated that he could still feel the ghost of her warmth on his arm, the almost-moment between them humming like a chord unresolved.
